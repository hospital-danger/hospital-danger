        Judy’s Story

        Judy Gaines hoped a knee replacement would make her life better. Instead, it has tortured her.

        After the July 2010 surgery, an infection set in and ran roughshod. Bacteria consumed bones and flesh in her leg until doctors had to remove her new artificial knee. They inserted antibiotic chips in the space where the knee should have been. For almost four months, a microbial battle waged in her leg.

        In that time, Gaines had no knee. She sat in a wheelchair, her left leg locked in a brace to keep the empty socket from folding in on itself. Her sister and best friend, Claire, helped care for her.

        Gaines couldn’t bend her leg to get in a car, so she needed an ambulance for doctor visits. Wheeling about home required wide berth – to avoid jarring the leg on a cabinet or chair, causing blinding pain.

        Once when Gaines was alone, she fell out of the wheelchair. She had to call emergency workers for help.

        Infections acquired at the hospital are one of the most common problems with knee replacements. Gaines realized she had one just days after she returned home. Doctors did emergency surgery to try and scrape the infection out. Months of physical therapy followed, but the infection persisted, eating away at the bone in her lower leg until it fractured in February 2011.

        Gaines spent the next five months in a wheelchair. She gamely spent more weeks in physical therapy ­– and was able to walk again – until the artificial knee became unstable, again from the infection, her medical records show. She couldn’t take a step without excruciating pain.

        It took many more months to find a surgeon with the skills to fix the problem. One doctor looked and said he would have to amputate, the sisters say. They did not go back.

        In May 2012, Gaines went to a new facility, the Hospital for Special Surgery in New York City. Doctors there said they could save her leg and that she would be able to walk again. That August, they removed the original knee replacement, and in November, put in a new knee and graft to replace lost bone.

        Gaines went home from rehab in January, only to suffer an accident in which she fell and hit her head, requiring another surgery. She is in therapy and learning to walk again, but is expected to fully recover.

        Among the lessons Gaines says she learned is that there’s no such thing as a routine surgery. “Any operation is dangerous,” she says. “It’s an assault to your body no matter how you look at it.”

        By the Numbers

        At an average hospital…

        • 25% of workers say staff do not freely speak up if they see something that could hurt a patient.
        • 47% of workers say staff work longer hours than is best for patient care.
        • 39% of workers say the management is only interested in patient safety after something goes wrong.
        • 24% of workers say their supervisor “overlooks patient safety problems that happen over and over.”
        • 36% of workers say patient safety is sacrificed to get more work done.
